Teaching Points For This Case

1. Provide adequate minute ventilation: Use caution when intubating a patient with metabolic acidosis who is compensating by increasing their minute ventilation prior to intubation (e.g. diabetic ketoacidosis, salicyclate toxicity, sepsis). Sedation, paralysis, and mechanical ventilation can rapidly decrease the patient's minute ventilation, allowing pH to fall, and leading to worsening acidosis.

2. Consider permissive hypercapnia: Permissive hypercapnia, a ventilatory strategy used to minimize lung injury in which the lungs are ventilated with a low inspiratory volume and pressure, has been accepted progressively in critical care for adult, pediatric, and neonatal patients requiring mechanical ventilation. Its limitations are the resulting hypoventilation, CO2 retention, acidosis, and hypoxemia. The progressive rise of PaCO2 should not exceed 10 mm Hg/h and arterial oxygen saturation PaO 2 should be maintained > 60 mm Hg.

1. Maintaining adequate oxygenation: The Brain Trauma Foundation recommends monitoring oxygenation and avoiding hypoxia, defined as PaO2 < 60 mm Hg and SpO 2 < 90%.

2. Avoid hypocarpnia and hypercapnia: Although hypercapnia and acidosis can compromise cerebral blood flow, prophylactic hyperventilation has been shown to be deleterious in patients with head injuries and is no longer recommended. The goal is to sustain eucapnia with a PaCO2 between 35 mm Hg and 45.

3. Complications: Respiratory complications are common in patients with traumatic brain injury since lung protective ventilatory strategies are generally avoided to maintain adequate oxygenation and eucapnia. Low-tidal-volume ventilation would lead to permissive hypercapnia and lower PaO2.

1. ARDSNet Protocol: The ARDSNet protocol involves reducing the tidal volume between 4 to 6 mL/kg IBW to achieve a plateau pressure of <=30 cm H 2 O. FiO 2 and PEEP are adjusted to achieve a goal of PaO 2 of 55 to 80 mm Hg or SpO 2 of 88% to 95%. In the ARDSNet study, pH was allowed to be as low as 7.15.

3. Beyond the ventilator: Additional strategies that have shown promise in patients with ARDS include prone positioning and the early administration of paralytics.

1. Avoid dynamic hyperinflation or auto-PEEP: Patients with obstructive airway disease have decreased elasticity as a result of lung tissue injury. Patients have a prolonged expiratory phase and their inspiratory to expiratory ratio is reduced (~1:4 or 1:5). Auto-PEEP can result in barotrauma, hypotensive, and cardiac arrest.
